CBT Thought Diaries

A CBT thought diary is a tool used in Cognitive Behavioral Therapy to help individuals recognize and challenge unhelpful thoughts linked to negative emotions or behaviors. By documenting specific thoughts in response to situations, users can better understand and modify their thinking patterns.

1
Initial Anxious Thought/Situation
This is the automatic thought or perception that comes to mind when faced with a specific situation or trigger. It's often driven by underlying beliefs and can be negative or distorted, leading to feelings of anxiety, sadness, or anger. For instance, upon making a minor mistake at work, one might think, "I'm terrible at my job."
2
What Kind of Cognitive Distortion Is This?
Cognitive distortions are biased or irrational ways of thinking that contribute to emotional distress. By identifying the type of distortion, individuals can better understand how they might be misinterpreting situations. Common distortions include "all-or-nothing thinking," "catastrophizing," and "overgeneralization." Using the previous example, thinking "I'm terrible at my job" after a minor mistake could be an "overgeneralization."
3
Evidence Against:
This step involves challenging the initial thought by seeking objective evidence that contradicts it. By looking at situations rationally and gathering contrary evidence, individuals can counteract their distorted beliefs. For the aforementioned example, evidence against the thought might include: "I've received positive feedback in the past," or "Everyone makes mistakes sometimes."
4
Rational Thought About Situation After Analyzing the Evidence:
After evaluating the evidence, individuals reformulate their initial thought into a more balanced and rational one. This helps in responding to situations more adaptively. For the earlier example, after considering the evidence, one might conclude: "While I made a mistake this time, it doesn't mean I'm bad at my job. I can learn from this and move on."
